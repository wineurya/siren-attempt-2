Project Summary

Siren stands out as a dating app that prioritizes user safety in the online dating scene. It's designed to preemptively identify potential risks, ensuring users can make informed decisions about their interactions. Through our innovative features like 'Red Flags' and mandatory video calls, Siren has demonstrated that safety can be seamlessly integrated into the dating experience. The result is an app where users can date more confidently and securely

Product Problem Space

The challenge with online dating has always been the balance between easy connections and safety. Traditional dating apps focus on quick matches, often overlooking user safety. Siren addresses this gap, particularly for young, casual daters. We hypothesized that incorporating clear safety signals on profiles and introducing mandatory video calls after a certain number of texts would enhance user trust and safety. These hypotheses were prioritized based on their potential impact on user safety and the feasibility of implementation.

Solution

Sprint 1 (Red Flags Feature):
 Our first sprint was dedicated to the 'Red Flags' feature. We created scenarios using fake profiles with varying levels of warnings â€“ from multiple red flags to none. The goal was to observe if and how these warnings influenced user interactions. The results were enlightening. Users generally avoided profiles with red flags and showed cautious interest in those with yellow flags. This feedback validated our assumption that clear warning signals could significantly impact user decision-making.

Sprint 1 (Going Forward):
 In response to user feedback, we refined the 'Red Flags' feature. We introduced a pull-out menu for report details, ensuring the information was accessible yet not overly prominent. This approach balanced the need for safety information with a non-intrusive user experience, addressing concerns that too much emphasis on reports might negatively affect user interactions.

Sprint 2 (Mandatory Video Calls):
 The second sprint focused on the mandatory video call feature. The premise was simple but powerful: require a video call after a set number of text messages. This was tested by simulating video calls between users and team members, with the latter portraying profiles with yellow or red flags. The aim was to see if users could identify deceptive behavior during these calls.

Sprint 2 (Scheduling):
 The insights gained were significant. Users were able to detect inconsistencies or deceptive behavior, leading to more informed decisions about whether to continue interactions. However, we also recognized the need for a more user-friendly approach to scheduling these calls. As a result, we implemented a warning system that alerted users about the approaching video call requirement, allowing them to plan these calls more conveniently. This adjustment not only enhanced the functionality of the feature but also respected user preferences for a more informal scheduling process.
